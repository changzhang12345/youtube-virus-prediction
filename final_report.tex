% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{center}\large\bfseries Abstract\end{center}
This project investigates whether a YouTube video's virality can be predicted
using only information available before publication: the video title, tags,
publish timestamp, content category, and description length.  We define a video
as viral if its view count reaches the 80th percentile among all videos in the
dataset, yielding a binary classification task with roughly 20\% positive
examples.  Two models are compared: a Logistic Regression baseline trained on
nine structured features, and an RBF Kernel SVM trained on the same structured
features augmented with 384-dimensional sentence embeddings of the video title
and tags.  The SVM achieves an AUC of 0.711 and an F1 score of 0.434, compared
to 0.599 and 0.352 for the baseline, suggesting that semantic content provides
meaningful additional predictive signal.  Analysis of the Logistic Regression
coefficients reveals that tag count and the presence of exclamation marks in
the title are strong positive predictors, while question marks are negative,
suggesting that emotionally-driven titles are associated with higher viral rates
than informational ones.  A
cross-category generalization experiment further reveals that viral patterns are
strongly category-specific, with in-domain F1 averaging 0.859 and cross-domain
F1 averaging 0.166, a finding that points toward category-aware modeling as a
natural direction for future work.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} viral prediction, sentence embeddings, logistic regression, kernel SVM, PCA

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Predicting whether a YouTube video will go viral is a problem of practical
interest to content creators, platform operators, and marketers.  Most
approaches to this problem rely on post-publication engagement signals such as
view counts, likes, and comment volume --- metrics that are only observable
after a video has already been released and seen by an audience.  This project
poses a stricter and more realistic question: can we predict virality
using only information that exists at the moment of upload?

The features considered are limited to the video title, tags, publish
timestamp, content category, and description length, all of which are
determined before a video goes live.  Engagement metrics (views, likes,
dislikes, comment count) are explicitly excluded as model inputs; view counts
are used solely to construct the binary viral label.

Beyond the core prediction task, the project investigates a second question:
do viral patterns generalize across content categories?  A model
trained on Entertainment videos may have learned patterns specific to that genre.
Testing whether those patterns transfer to Music or Comedy reveals
whether virality is a universal phenomenon or a category-specific one --- a
question with direct implications for how such models should be designed and
deployed in practice.

Two main experiments are conducted.  Experiment~1 trains a Logistic Regression
classifier on structured features only to establish a baseline and to identify
which individual variables carry the most predictive weight.  Experiment~2
augments these features with sentence embeddings of the video title and tags,
encoding their semantic content into a dense 384-dimensional vector, and trains
an RBF Kernel SVM to test whether the meaning of a title carries additional
predictive power beyond the structured features.  Two further experiments
visualize the learned feature space via PCA and measure cross-category
generalization through a held-out F1 matrix.

% ── 2. Data ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data}

\subsection{Dataset}

The dataset used is the \textbf{Kaggle Trending YouTube Video Statistics} (US
subset), consisting of a CSV file of video records and a JSON category mapping
file.  It contains metadata for videos that appeared on YouTube's US trending
page, including the video title, tags, publish timestamp, category identifier,
description text, view count, like count, dislike count, and comment count.
The raw dataset contains 40,949 rows.  Because the same video can appear on
multiple trending days, we deduplicate by video identifier and retain only the
first appearance of each video, reducing the dataset to 6,351 unique videos.
After removing rows with missing titles or unparseable publish timestamps, all
6,351 samples are retained.

\subsection{Label Definition}

A binary viral label is constructed from view counts:
\[
  \text{viral} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } \text{views} \geq Q_{0.80}(\text{views}) \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\]
where $Q_{0.80}$ denotes the 80th percentile of the view count distribution
(933,053 views).  This yields approximately 20\% positive (viral) examples,
providing a more balanced class distribution for model training.

\subsection{Category Distribution}

The viral rate varies substantially across content categories, as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:viral_by_category}.  Gaming leads with a 40.8\% viral rate,
while News \& Politics and Pets \& Animals fall below 6\%.  This strong
category-level variation suggests that content category is an important
predictor, a hypothesis confirmed by the feature importance analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:results}.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{phase1_viral_rate_by_category.png}
  \caption{Viral rate broken down by YouTube content category.
           Gaming exhibits the highest viral rate at 40.8\%, while
           News \& Politics and Pets \& Animals fall below 6\%.}

\end{figure}

% ── 3. Methodology ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

Two feature sets are constructed from the allowed pre-publication variables.

\textbf{Structured features} (9 dimensions):
\begin{itemize}
  \item publish hour --- hour of day (0--23) extracted from the publish timestamp
  \item publish weekday --- day of week (0 = Monday, 6 = Sunday)
  \item title length --- number of characters in the title
  \item title word count --- number of whitespace-delimited tokens in the title
  \item title has exclamation --- binary indicator for the presence of ``!''
  \item title has question --- binary indicator for the presence of ``?''
  \item tag count --- number of tags associated with the video
  \item description length --- character length of the video description
  \item category id --- integer category identifier
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Semantic embedding features:}  To capture the meaning of the video
title and tags, we concatenate the title text with the cleaned tag string and
encode the result using the pretrained sentence transformer model
\textit{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}, producing a 384-dimensional dense embedding per
video.  Unlike bag-of-words approaches such as TF-IDF, sentence embeddings
capture semantic similarity and contextual meaning, allowing the model to
recognize that ``Epic Fails Compilation'' and ``Best Bloopers Ever'' carry
similar signals even without lexical overlap.

The full feature matrix concatenates the scaled structured features and the
sentence embedding, producing a 393-dimensional dense representation per video.
To prevent data leakage, the standard scaler for structured features and the
PCA transform used in Experiment~2 are fitted exclusively on the training split
and then applied to the test split.  The sentence transformer model is
pretrained and frozen; it is not fine-tuned on this dataset.

\subsection{Train/Test Split}

The dataset is divided into a 70\% training set (4,445 samples) and a 30\%
test set (1,906 samples) using stratified sampling (preserving the viral rate
in both splits) with a fixed random seed for reproducibility.

\subsection{Experiment 1 --- Logistic Regression Baseline}
\label{sec:lr}

Logistic Regression with $\ell_2$ regularization is trained on the nine
structured features only.  The model minimizes the regularized cross-entropy
loss:
\[
  \min_{w,b} \;\sum_{i=1}^{n}
    \log\!\left(1 + e^{-y_i(w^\top x_i + b)}\right)
  + \frac{1}{2C}\|w\|^2
\]
where $C > 0$ controls regularization strength.  Class imbalance is handled by
reweighting each training example inversely proportional to its class
frequency.  The optimal $C$ is selected via 5-fold stratified cross-validation
over the grid $C \in \{0.001,\ 0.01,\ 0.1,\ 1,\ 10\}$, with F1 score as the
selection criterion.  Because Logistic Regression produces interpretable
coefficients, this experiment also serves as a feature importance analysis.

\subsection{Experiment 2 --- RBF Kernel SVM}

A Support Vector Machine with the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel is trained
on the full 393-dimensional feature set.  To improve computational efficiency,
PCA first reduces the feature matrix to 100 principal components (retaining
97.33\% of total variance).  The RBF kernel implicitly maps inputs into a
high-dimensional feature space:
\[
  K(x_i, x_j) = \exp\!\left(-\gamma\,\|x_i - x_j\|^2\right)
\]
The SVM then finds the maximum-margin separating hyperplane in this space.
Hyperparameters $C \in \{0.1,\,1,\,10\}$ and
$\gamma \in \{\text{scale},\,0.01,\,0.1\}$ are jointly tuned via 5-fold
cross-validation optimizing F1.  Class imbalance is handled identically to
Experiment~1.

\subsection{Experiment 3 --- PCA Visualization}

To examine the geometry of the feature space, PCA is applied to project the
full training feature matrix to 2 dimensions.  Viral and non-viral samples are
plotted separately to assess visual separability, both for the full training
set and individually for four representative categories: Gaming, Music,
Entertainment, and Science \& Technology.  These four categories are selected
to maximize visual contrast --- Gaming has the highest viral rate (40.8\%) and
Music the lowest cross-domain transfer, making them informative anchors for
understanding the feature space.  Note that Experiment~4 instead uses the four
highest-frequency categories (Entertainment, Music, Howto \& Style, Comedy) to
ensure sufficient per-category training data.

\subsection{Experiment 4 --- Cross-Category Generalization}

To assess whether viral patterns transfer across content genres, the four most
frequent categories are selected: Entertainment, Music, Howto \& Style, and
Comedy.  For each ordered pair (train category, test category), a separate SVM
is trained on all samples from the training category and evaluated on all
samples from the test category using the best hyperparameters from
Experiment~2.  This produces a $4\times4$ F1 matrix whose diagonal entries
represent in-domain performance and whose off-diagonal entries represent
cross-domain generalization.

% ── 5. Results ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experiment 1 --- Logistic Regression Baseline}

Logistic Regression trained on the nine structured features achieves an AUC of
0.599 and an F1 of 0.352.  Cross-validation selected $C = 0.1$ as the best
regularization parameter.  Table~\ref{tab:coef} shows the learned coefficient
weights sorted by absolute magnitude.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Logistic Regression feature coefficients (sorted by absolute value).
           Positive values indicate association with virality.}
  \label{tab:coef}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Coefficient} & \textbf{Direction} \\
    \midrule
    category id           & $-0.308$ & category-dependent \\
    tag count             & $+0.236$ & more tags $\to$ more viral \\
    title word count      & $-0.178$ & shorter titles more viral \\
    publish weekday       & $+0.164$ & later in week more viral \\
    description length    & $+0.119$ & longer descriptions more viral \\
    title has exclamation & $+0.112$ & ``!'' associated with virality \\
    title has question    & $-0.066$ & ``?'' negatively associated \\
    publish hour          & $-0.059$ & \\
    title length          & $+0.001$ & negligible \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Content category is the single strongest predictor, consistent with the large
variation in viral rates across categories observed in Figure~\ref{fig:viral_by_category}.
Tag count ranks second: videos with more tags tend to go viral more often,
likely because thorough tagging reflects creator experience and improves
discoverability within the recommendation system.  Description length carries
a similar interpretation --- more effort invested in the description signals
a more professional production.

A notable finding concerns punctuation in titles.  The presence of an
exclamation mark is positively associated with virality ($+0.112$), while a
question mark is negatively associated ($-0.066$).  This contrast reflects a
difference in content style: exclamation marks appear in emotionally-driven,
entertainment-oriented titles (``You Won't Believe This!''), while question
marks appear in informational or tutorial titles (``How do you fix this?''),
which tend to belong to lower-viral-rate categories such as Howto \& Style
and Education.

The model achieves a recall of 0.570, identifying 57\% of viral videos,
but at low precision (0.255), indicating a substantial false-positive rate.
The AUC of 0.599 is modestly above random chance, establishing the ceiling of
structured features alone and motivating the semantic features in
Experiment~2.  Figure~\ref{fig:lr} shows the ROC curve and confusion matrix.

  \centering

  \caption{Logistic Regression baseline: ROC curve (left) and confusion
           matrix (right) on the held-out test set.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Experiment 2 --- Kernel SVM}

Adding 384-dimensional sentence embeddings of the video title and tags to the
nine structured features, reducing to 100 PCA components, and training an RBF
Kernel SVM yields substantial improvement across all metrics.
Cross-validation selected $C = 10$ and $\gamma = 0.1$.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Test-set performance of the two models.}
  \label{tab:results}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Precision} &
    \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1} & \textbf{AUC} \\
    \midrule
    LR Baseline (structured only)            & 0.581 & 0.255 & 0.570 & 0.352 & 0.599 \\
    Kernel SVM (structured + embeddings)     & 0.732 & 0.375 & 0.514 & 0.434 & 0.711 \\
    \midrule
    \rowcolor{gray!15}
    Absolute improvement ($\Delta$)          & +0.151 & +0.120 & $-0.056$ & +0.082 & +0.112 \\
    \rowcolor{gray!15}
    Relative improvement (\%)               & +26.0\% & +47.1\% & $-9.8\%$ & +23.3\% & +18.7\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

AUC rises from 0.599 to 0.711 (+18.7\%) and F1 from 0.352 to 0.434 (+23.3\%).
Accuracy and precision improve substantially.  Recall decreases slightly
(0.570 $\to$ 0.514): the LR baseline, faced with a class-imbalanced problem,
casts a wide net and flags many videos as viral, achieving high recall at the
cost of low precision (0.255).  The SVM, operating in a richer feature space,
learns a more selective decision boundary --- it misses slightly more true
viral videos but is far less likely to misclassify non-viral ones, yielding
much higher precision (0.375) and a better overall F1.  This trade-off is
appropriate for a task where false positives (predicting viral incorrectly)
carry their own cost.  The AUC of 0.711 means that when given one viral and
one non-viral video drawn at random, the model ranks the viral one higher
71.1\% of the time --- a meaningful result given that the prediction relies
solely on pre-publication information.

The gap between the two models suggests that semantic content contributes useful
predictive signal, although a stricter ablation study would be needed to fully
separate the effect of embeddings from the effect of the more flexible nonlinear
SVM model.  The structured features alone (AUC 0.599) capture operational
signals such as category, tag count, and timing, while the full model
additionally encodes \textit{what} the title says.

Figure~\ref{fig:svm} shows the ROC comparison, SVM confusion matrix, and a
side-by-side metric bar chart.

\subsection{Experiment 3 --- PCA Visualization}

Projecting the 393-dimensional training features to 3 principal components
retains 46.3\% of total variance (PC1: 20.6\%, PC2: 15.3\%, PC3: 10.4\%).  The dense, high-rank structure
of sentence embeddings means that no small number of principal components can
capture the full feature space, so some class overlap in the projected view is
expected.  Figure~\ref{fig:pca_overall} shows the 3D projection from two
viewing angles: viral and non-viral videos overlap considerably, consistent
with the inherent difficulty of the task, but viral videos are not uniformly
distributed --- they concentrate toward one end of PC1, confirming that the
learned features carry genuine discriminative signal even if it is not
concentrated in just two or three directions.

\subsection{Experiment 4 --- Cross-Category Generalization}

Training and testing separate SVM models within and across the four most
frequent categories (Entertainment, Music, Howto \& Style, Comedy) yields the
$4 \times 4$ F1 matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{phase6_cross_category_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Cross-category F1 matrix.  Rows indicate the training category;
           columns indicate the test category.  Diagonal entries are in-domain
           results; off-diagonal entries are cross-domain results.}
  \label{fig:heatmap}
\end{figure}

The diagonal (in-domain) entries average 0.859, confirming that
category-specific models learn strong within-genre patterns.  Individual
in-domain F1 scores are high: Comedy 0.877, Howto \& Style 0.877, Music 0.864,
and Entertainment 0.817.  The off-diagonal (cross-domain) entries average only
0.166, a drop of 0.693.  The most extreme case is Howto \& Style trained on
Music: F1 = 0.000, a complete failure.

This large gap reveals that viral patterns are fundamentally category-specific.
A model trained on Comedy learns to recognize comedic framing and humor-driven
titles; applied to Music, those signals provide no useful information about
whether a track will trend.  The finding is not that the models are weak --- on
their own categories they perform very well --- but that the concept of
``viral'' has a different linguistic fingerprint in each genre.

The higher in-domain F1 scores in this experiment should be interpreted
carefully: each category-specific model is trained and evaluated on a narrower
within-genre distribution, which may be an easier task than the global
held-out test setting.  The in-domain F1 of 0.859 thus reflects within-category
predictability rather than an improvement over the global model.

% ── 6. Discussion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Viral video prediction from pre-publication features is feasible.}
The Kernel SVM achieves an AUC of 0.711 using only pre-publication information
--- no view counts, likes, or any post-publication engagement signal.  This
performance is reasonable for a noisy social-media prediction task, especially
given that the model relies solely on pre-publication information.  The
structured-only baseline (AUC 0.599) already exceeds chance meaningfully, and
the addition of semantic embeddings pushes performance substantially higher.

\textbf{Semantic content contributes meaningful predictive signal.}  The 18.7\%
AUC gain from adding sentence embeddings suggests that \textit{what} a creator
writes in their title and tags carries predictive signal beyond timing and
category, though a full ablation study separating embedding contribution from
model flexibility would be needed to confirm this precisely.  Title wording is
a concrete lever available to creators before publication, and optimizing it
may help improve predicted reach.

\textbf{Operational features reveal creator professionalism.}  Among the
structured features, tag count and description length are the second and fifth
strongest predictors respectively.  Both serve as proxies for how much effort
and care a creator invests in their upload workflow.  Experienced creators who
carefully tag their videos and write detailed descriptions are more likely to
produce content that eventually trends, reflecting not just content quality but
also platform literacy.

\textbf{Punctuation in titles carries interpretable signal.}  Exclamation
marks are positively associated with virality while question marks are
negatively associated.  This asymmetry reflects a stylistic divide between
emotionally-charged entertainment content and informational or tutorial content
--- the former tends to go viral more often.  Such findings illustrate that
even simple text features, when interpreted carefully, can reveal genuine
patterns in audience behavior.

\textbf{Viral patterns are category-specific, which is itself a useful
finding.}  The cross-category experiment was designed to ask whether a single
global model would be sufficient.  The answer is clearly no: in-domain F1
averages 0.859 while cross-domain F1 averages only 0.166.  This is not a
failure of the modeling approach; it is a discovery about the nature of
virality on YouTube.  Different genres have different linguistic cultures, and
what makes a Comedy title go viral has little in common with what makes a Music
video trend.  Knowing this guides how future systems should be built ---
with separate, category-aware models rather than a single one-size-fits-all
classifier.

\textbf{Limitations.}  The category identifier is treated as a raw integer
rather than one-hot encoded, which imposes an arbitrary ordinal structure on a
nominal variable; one-hot encoding would be more principled.  Additionally,
the dataset covers only US trending videos from a limited time window, which
may limit generalizability to other regions or periods.

\textbf{Future directions.}  Natural extensions include training dedicated
per-category models to leverage the strong in-domain performance observed here,
incorporating channel-level features such as historical viral rate as a proxy
for creator reputation, and exploring thumbnail image features using vision
models, since the thumbnail is a key driver of click-through rate that this
project did not address.

% ── 7. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates that predicting YouTube video virality from
pre-publication information alone is feasible.  By combining nine structured
metadata features with 384-dimensional sentence embeddings of the video title
and tags, and training an RBF Kernel SVM, we achieve an AUC of 0.711 and an F1
score of 0.434 on a held-out test set --- a 23.3\% improvement in F1 and an
18.7\% improvement in AUC over the structured-only Logistic Regression baseline.

Feature importance analysis on the baseline model reveals that content category
is the strongest single predictor, followed by tag count and the presence of
exclamation marks in the title.  The contrast between exclamation marks
(positive) and question marks (negative) highlights a meaningful stylistic
divide between viral entertainment content and non-viral informational content.

The cross-category generalization experiment adds a deeper insight: viral
patterns are not universal but strongly genre-dependent, with in-domain F1
averaging 0.859 and cross-domain F1 averaging just 0.166.  Rather than
undermining the project's success, this finding clarifies the right modeling
strategy going forward --- category-aware models that respect the distinct
linguistic norms of each genre.  Together, these results demonstrate that
machine learning can extract meaningful structure from simple pre-publication
metadata, with practical implications for content strategy, platform
recommendation design, and creator tooling.
